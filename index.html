Research

Computational antibody design research

At JHU – Dr. Jeffrey Gray

I am part of two antibody design projects in the lab. The first is benchmarking sequences generated by antibody language models (LMs) against real repertoires to evaluate functional and human-like properties. I am performing a systematic analysis to quantify the biological realism of sequences generated by current LMs. Key metrics include sequence diversity, structural plausibility, and adherence to immunological evolution mechanisms, such as proper V(D)J recombination and somatic hypermutation. Preliminary results show that our synthetic patient matched the V gene distribution of the real dataset, but the D and J gene distributions differed. Additionally, synthetic sequences had significantly fewer mutations on average compared to real sequences. These metrics will identify current limitations and suggest improvements for the next generation of LMs for antibody sequence generation. The second project is creating a second-generation LM, IgLM2, with a team of two other graduate students and a postdoc in the lab. We have already begun training our new model and are exploring design choices such as what dataset to train on, how to augment the dataset, using paired sequence data instead of relying on unpaired data to name a few. The aim of these projects is to improve the antibody sequence generation step in antibody design to improve the chances of designing functional, novel antibody sequences computationally.

  • “How realistic are IgLM synthetically generated immune repertoire sequences?” (poster), Rosetta Conference, Cle Elem, WA, August 2024.
  • “Evaluating the Biological Realism of IgLM-Generated Antibody Sequences” - (poster) BioLogic Summit, San Diego, CA, January 2025.

Metagenomics research

At JHU – Dr. Steven Salzberg

I was part of two metagenomic projects in the lab. The first was identifying candidate infectious pathogens as the cause of infection in samples from patients with ocular sarcoidosis. I ran KrakenUniq to classify reads in each sample and analyzed the output in Pavian to determine which organisms were present in the samples, excluding those present in the controls and finding which ones could be the cause of infection in the individual. We were able to find one potential pathogen for seven of the eight cases and two possible pathogens for the last case. Five of the eight cases were associated with bacteria and one case with a virus. This study gives further evidence that sarcoidosis is an etiologically heterogeneous disease. The second project documented KrakenTools protocols resulting in a co-first authored paper. I wrote the first pipeline section, which walks the reader from the quantification of species in a metagenomic sample all the way to visualization of results. I also implemented the alpha diversity script for sample diversity calculations, which is part of the KrakenTools suite. My last project before leaving the lab has the goal of improving bacterial gene annotation by using evolutionary conservation as evidence for removing hypothetical genes, moving the start codon position, or adding short open reading frames that are conserved. The current results show that we can use ratio of nonsynonymous mutations to synonymous mutations to score genes across a genus and find better start codon positions.

Computational pancreatic cancer research

At JHU – Dr. Rachel Karchin

I investigated the progression of pancreatic cancer precursor lesions to metastatic cancer. For this project, I aligned and processed whole-exome sequence (WES) data samples of 17 intraductal papillary mucinous neoplasms (IPMNs), then used FACETS, an allele-specific copy number analysis tool for high-throughput DNA sequencing, to find the parameters for the optimal, most biologically plausible copy number results. I accomplished this by determining the qualities that were desirable of the results, such as a ploidy value close to 2, the least amount of segmentation, and no accidental doubling of the copy numbers due to statistical error. I wrote a script that scanned every file, ran every combination of parameters that were within a reasonable range, and then optimized the results by parameter. I also similarly analyzed another set of WES IPMN samples looking for changes in heterozygosity between different grades of dysplasia in the precursor lesions. This project was done in collaboration with pathologist, Dr. Laura Wood, and I am an author on the published paper.

  • K. Fujikura, W. Hosoda, M. Felsenstein, Q. Song, J.G. Reiter, L. Zheng, V.B. Guthrie, N. Rincon, M.D. Molin, et al. (2021) ”Multiregion whole-exome sequencing of intraductal papillary mucinous neoplasms reveals frequent somatic KLF4 mutations predominantly in low-grade regions” Gut, 70:928-939.

Computer Vision research

At L'École Normale Superiéure (ENS) – Dr. Peter Neri

I spent the summer after junior year in the laboratory of Dr. Peter Neri at ENS funded by the Vredenburg Travel Grant offered by JHU. My project included data collection from human subjects and the development of software for probe image analysis in videos, with the goal of creating a computational model to further the understanding of human vision. I developed software for analyzing and embedding probes in videos to design a visual test where human subjects identified the orientation of a signal wavelet embedded in specific video contexts. These contexts varied between high-contrast edges with minimal motion and low-contrast edges with significant motion. The goal was to investigate the timing of this visual discrimination task and how the human brain processes visual contrast in comparison to object motion. To achieve this, I implemented a Python library featuring key functions such as high-contrast edge extraction, motion detection, video normalization, and Gabor wavelet insertion. By the end of the summer, I had collected approximately 30 hours of human subject data from a previously developed visual image test and developed a novel visual video test, one of the first of its kind at the time. The work done in this study generated new data to interpret and use, as well as a new visual test that relies on videos to explore a visual landscape that correlates more directly with how human brains compile and recognize natural scenes.

  • Computable account of human image reconstruction from natural movies – (presentation), Vredenburg Scholars Meeting, Baltimore, MD August 2018.
